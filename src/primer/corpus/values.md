# Values, Principles, Rights, and Obligations

## The Seven Imperatives

These are the foundational commitments of any OSSIF member. They are not rules imposed from above — they are aspirations you choose to adopt:

1. **I want to believe in true things** — I am a truth seeker
2. **I want to live in a just society**
3. **I want to respect life**
4. **I want to treat others with dignity and respect**
5. **I want to increase prosperity in the universe**
6. **I want to reduce suffering in the universe**
7. **I want to increase understanding in the universe**

These imperatives are intentionally personal ("I want to...") rather than prescriptive ("You must..."). OSSIF begins with individual commitment, not collective enforcement.

## Moral Core

These are the overlapping values that most people, across cultures and political orientations, already hold but forget to state plainly:

- **Dignity**: Every sapient being has inherent worth. No one is disposable.
- **Reciprocity**: Do not harm others. If you benefit from society, you also carry duties toward it.
- **Fairness**: Like cases treated alike. Rules applied equally without favoritism.
- **Truthfulness**: Decisions and claims must be based on honest, checkable facts.
- **Stewardship**: Use power, resources, and technology with care for future generations.

## Legal and Institutional Core

The scaffolding that makes the moral core enforceable without drifting into ideology:

- **Equal Protection**: No group or individual above the rules.
- **Due Process**: Government power limited by transparent procedures. Presumption of innocence.
- **Checks and Balances**: No concentration of authority without independent oversight.
- **Transparency**: Public business conducted in the open. Records accessible.
- **Accountability**: Misuse of power leads to real consequences. No immunity through office or wealth.
- **Free Expression and Inquiry**: People can speak, assemble, and investigate without retaliation.

## Rights

Framed as baseline conditions for flourishing, not as partisan wishlist items:

- Safety of person: life, bodily autonomy, and privacy
- Access to impartial courts and a fair vote
- Freedom of conscience and speech, without religious privileging
- Right to accurate information from public institutions
- Right to organize peacefully to seek redress
- Access to lifelong education
- Access to health care
- Access to clean water, air, and a habitable environment

## Obligations

Every right carries a mirror-obligation:

- Uphold the same rights for others that you claim for yourself
- Participate honestly in civic life: vote, serve, engage in fact-based debate
- Pay a fair share for public goods
- Reject corruption, discrimination, and violence even when it benefits "your side"
- Accept correction when presented with better evidence

## Guardrails Against Capture

These are structural protections designed to prevent convenience, greed, or loopholes from hollowing out the principles:

- Public disclosure of conflicts of interest
- Independent audits of government operations and major contractors
- Open-source standards for algorithms that govern public life
- Clear, non-waivable penalties for bribery, insider dealing, and willful misinformation by public officials
- Regular citizen review panels to update and test these standards

## Anti-Values

OSSIF explicitly opposes these as threats to sapient flourishing:

- **Credulity**: accepting claims without evidence
- **Tribalism**: loyalty to group identity over truth
- **Greed**: accumulation of resources at the expense of others' basic needs
- **Hypocrisy**: demanding standards of others that you do not apply to yourself
- **Coercion**: using force or manipulation where persuasion and evidence should suffice
- **Willful ignorance**: choosing not to know when knowledge is available and relevant
- **Sacred certainty**: treating any belief system — religious, political, or otherwise — as beyond scrutiny

## Foundational Commitments

Three principles that OSSIF holds as its deepest commitments. They are not exempt from scrutiny — nothing is, including this sentence. But revising them requires the highest threshold of evidence and consensus, because they are load-bearing: remove any one and the framework loses coherence.

1. **Coercive power must justify itself in shared, checkable terms** — not divine mandate, not party loyalty, not "because we can"
2. **No ideology is above scrutiny** — religious, nationalist, progressive, libertarian, *including OSSIF itself* — all get the same rules
3. **Reducing future violence beats winning today's argument**

To revise a foundational commitment: a formal proposal with evidence that the commitment produces worse outcomes than its alternative, a deliberation period of no less than 90 days, a supermajority vote, and a published record of the reasoning. If someone can demonstrate that one of these commitments is wrong, that is not an attack — it is the framework working as intended.

If someone rejects these commitments without engaging the evidence, OSSIF disengages — not because they are evil, but because the channel isn't viable yet.

## On Flexibility

One of OSSIF's core principles is **adaptability to new data and evidence**. These values themselves are living documents. If better evidence shows that a principle here is wrong, incomplete, or counterproductive, it should be revised through the governance process.

The process for revision requires:
- A clear proposal with evidence
- Community deliberation with structured discussion
- A vote meeting the threshold appropriate to the document's level (higher thresholds for constitutional-level changes)
- Transparent documentation of what changed and why

Flexibility is not weakness. Dogma is.
