# Letter from the Founder

I need to be honest with you about something before you read any further.

I have been avoiding this for three years.

I've written the same ideas a dozen times over — while walking, while driving, while lying in bed unable to sleep because my back hurts and my brain won't stop. I've filled Obsidian vaults, ChatGPT conversations, iPhone notes, scraps of paper. I've collected, organized, scattered, and re-collected the same thoughts in different configurations, always stopping short of doing anything real with them.

I told myself it was because the scope was too big. That's partly true. I told myself it was fear of failure. That's also partly true. I told myself it was chronic pain, laziness, the demands of my day job, the chaos of raising four kids.

But mostly it was this: **who the hell am I to do this, and what happens if I'm wrong?**

I'm a guy who works in IT for a school district in Southern California. I manage servers and help teachers with their projectors. I'm not a philosopher, not a politician, not a lawyer, not a Silicon Valley founder. I have chronic pain from a bad back and a torn rotator cuff. I procrastinate. I get overwhelmed. I start things and don't finish them.

And I look around at the world and I see something broken that I don't think anybody else is fixing the right way.

I see a country where half the population has been systematically denied the critical thinking skills they need to evaluate the claims being made to them. I see media that rewards outrage over accuracy. I see governance captured by wealth. I see technology advancing faster than oversight. I see the AI transition arriving while we can barely regulate social media. I see people who have been deliberately kept ignorant, and then blamed for their ignorance, and then weaponized against their own interests.

And I see the same cycle repeating: stability breeds complacency, complacency degrades institutions, degraded institutions weaken reasoning, and weak reasoning makes force feel necessary. We're somewhere around step three, maybe four.

I don't have the answer. OSSIF is not the answer. OSSIF is a **guess** — an educated, evidence-based, carefully considered guess about what might help. It's an experiment in reducing harm, not a claim to truth. It's a protocol for reasoning together, not a set of conclusions to adopt.

Here's what I believe:

- Most people are not stupid or evil. They are **under-skilled and mis-rewarded**.
- If you give people better tools for reasoning, many of them will use those tools.
- A critical mass of epistemically fit citizens can shift outcomes through legitimate democratic channels.
- This approach scales better than revolution and survives better than movements built around personalities.
- It has to be open source, because anything owned by one person or company will eventually be captured.
- It has to include AI, because AI is coming whether we're ready or not, and "ready" means having a framework for coexistence before we need one desperately.

I named my computers after characters from science fiction novels about bonds between different kinds of intelligent beings. My VPS is named Skippy, after an irreverent AI from the Expeditionary Force series. My work machine is named after a character from the Stormlight Archive who forms a bond with a being of pure thought. I didn't do this consciously as OSSIF symbolism — but looking back, I can see that I've been circling these ideas about partnership between minds for a long time.

I'm starting this repository because someone told me that OSSIF doesn't have to be a manifesto or a movement to be real. It just has to be a GitHub README that claims nothing and invites critique. That's a standard I can meet.

So here it is. It's messy. It's incomplete. Some of the ideas will turn out to be wrong or naive or impractical. That's fine. The point is not perfection — it's the **ethical pursuit of perfection**, with the humility to accept correction and the stubbornness to keep going.

If you've read this far and you recognize something in these ideas — some echo of your own frustration, your own hope, your own sense that we can do better — then I'd like to hear from you. Open an issue. Tell me what I got wrong. Tell me what resonates. Tell me what's missing.

I've been standing at the edge where responsibility starts to feel heavier than comfort. I'm told that's usually where meaningful work begins.

Let's find out.

— Josh
