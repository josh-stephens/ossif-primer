# Contributing to OSSIF

## The Most Important Rule

Bring evidence. If you disagree with something, explain why with checkable claims. If you propose something new, show your reasoning. "I feel like..." is a starting point for conversation. "Here's the evidence that..." is what changes the documents.

## How to Participate

### Reading and thinking
That counts. You don't need to write code or submit pull requests to participate in OSSIF. Understanding the framework and applying its reasoning tools in your own life is the most fundamental form of contribution.

### Opening issues
Found an error? See a gap? Disagree with a position? Open a GitHub issue. Be specific about what you think is wrong and why. Include evidence where possible. Even "this section is confusing" is useful feedback.

### Proposing changes
Fork the repository, make your changes, and submit a pull request. For content changes, your PR should include:

- What you changed
- Why you changed it
- What evidence supports the change
- What tradeoffs the change introduces

### Building tools
The Primer, the Avatar Portal, the Trust Token system, and other technology components described in [technology.md](docs/technology.md) are designs waiting for builders. If you want to implement something, open an issue first to discuss your approach.

### Translating
OSSIF should be accessible in every language. If you can translate any document, that's an enormously valuable contribution.

### Teaching
If you use OSSIF principles in a classroom, workshop, community meeting, or conversation — and you learn something about what works and what doesn't — share that. Open an issue with your observations.

## Document Change Process

Changes to documents have different thresholds depending on their level:

- **Typos, formatting, broken links**: Just submit a PR. These are maintenance.
- **Clarifications and examples**: Submit a PR with a brief explanation.
- **New content or significant rewording**: Open an issue for discussion first, then submit a PR.
- **Changes to core values or principles**: These require broader community discussion and a higher consensus threshold. Open an issue, allow time for deliberation, and document the reasoning thoroughly.

## Code of Conduct

Follow the Seven Imperatives. Specifically:

- Engage in good faith — assume others are trying to help, not attack
- Use evidence and reasoning, not personal attacks
- Respect the time and effort of other contributors
- Accept that you might be wrong — and be willing to update your position
- Don't use OSSIF spaces to recruit for other causes, sell products, or promote yourself

## What We Don't Accept

- Changes that violate the core values without evidence-based justification
- Content that dehumanizes any group or individual
- Unfalsifiable claims presented as established fact
- Vandalism, spam, or deliberate bad-faith disruption (arguments that the framework is fundamentally wrong are not just allowed — they're essential. Sabotage is not the same as dissent.)

## The Fork Invitation

OSSIF claims to be a method, not just a set of conclusions. If that's true, someone should be able to apply the method faithfully and arrive at different policy positions than the ones in [platform.md](docs/platform.md).

**We explicitly invite this.** Fork the repository, keep the epistemological commitments (evidence, falsifiability, steelmanning, the SIFT Check), and build a platform that reaches different conclusions. If you can do it rigorously, you've either proved that OSSIF's method works as advertised — or you've exposed that our conclusions were baked in from the start. Either outcome is valuable.

A fork that contradicts our platform through better reasoning is not a threat. It's the highest form of contribution.

## Questions?

Open an issue with the label "question". There are no stupid questions — only ones that haven't been asked yet.
